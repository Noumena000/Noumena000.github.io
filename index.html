Flagship Educational AI Project

Make understanding visible through dialogue.

The Socratic Assessment Platform is a faculty-centered research prototype that helps learners demonstrate what they understand by defining ideas, explaining reasons, applying concepts, answering objections, and revising their thinking under structured questioning.

How It Works

Assessment that asks students to show their thinking.

A polished written answer can conceal uncertainty, imitation, or shallow understanding. The platform adds a structured dialogue layer that gives learners opportunities to clarify, justify, apply, defend, and revise their ideas.

01

Prompt

An instructor defines the concept, task, or ethical problem to be explored.

02

Dialogue

The learner responds to questions that probe definitions, reasons, applications, objections, and revisions.

03

Review

The instructor receives organized evidence of reasoning while retaining responsibility for evaluation.

Why It Matters

Move beyond the question, “Did the student use AI?”

The more important educational question is whether the learner can explain, defend, apply, and revise an idea. The Socratic Assessment Platform is designed around that question.

It does not treat AI as an autonomous grader. It treats dialogue as evidence for faculty interpretation and judgment.

Designed for

  • Philosophy and ethics courses
  • Writing-intensive and general education courses
  • Nursing and health-sciences education
  • Critical thinking and AI-literacy instruction
  • Faculty development and assessment research

Faculty Pilot

Seeking a small number of instructional partners.

The next phase is a limited, low-stakes pilot with individual instructors who want to explore whether structured dialogue can reveal student understanding more clearly than a written submission alone.

A pilot can begin with one instructor, one course, and one formative assessment. Student work remains subject to faculty review, and the platform is presented as a research prototype rather than an autonomous grading system.

A pilot may examine

  • Quality of reasoning evidence
  • Instructor usefulness and workload
  • Student experience and perceived fairness
  • Opportunities for revision and learning
  • Privacy, accessibility, and governance needs

About

Scholar, educator, and builder.

I am Benjamin A. Moss, an Associate Professor of Philosophy, author, and independent software developer working at the intersection of applied ethics, critical thinking, AI literacy, educational technology, and human-centered software design.

My work asks how technology can deepen thought without replacing human judgment, responsibility, or original inquiry.

Core themes

  • Socratic pedagogy and formative assessment
  • Critical thinking and intellectual responsibility
  • AI literacy and responsible technology use
  • Applied ethics and human flourishing
  • Human-centered learning design

Research Vision

Reasoning, judgment, and humane technology.
